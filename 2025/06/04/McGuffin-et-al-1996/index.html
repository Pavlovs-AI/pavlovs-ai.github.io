McGuffin et al. (1996)

  • 2025-06-04

Aim

To investigate the concordance rates for Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) in monozygotic (MZ) and dizygotic (DZ) twins.

Participants

177 twin probands registered in London as suffering from MDD.

Procedures

  1. The twins were assessed by clinicians via a series of tests and interviews by blind researchers.
  2. The blind researchers were also blind as to whether each twin had MDD.
  3. The data also consisted of the twinsâ€™ medical records.

Results

  1. 46% concordance rate for MZ twins and 20% for DZ twins.
  2. Shorter periods of depression in one MZ twin was matched by similar depression in their twin sibling.
  3. MZ twins aged 65+ showed heightened levels of MDD compared to the general population.

Conclusions

  1. MDD may be highly heritable.
  2. Short-term MDD in one MZ twin appears to increase the probability of their MZ twin sibling to develop MDD.

Evaluation

  1. Longitudinal, high construct validity as participants could be tracked over time
  2. Blind researchers reduce researcher or conformation bias, higher internal validity
  3. Concordance rate for MZ twins was only 46%, indicating that environmental factors also play a crucial role
  4. Conducted prior to DNA testing, reduce reliability
