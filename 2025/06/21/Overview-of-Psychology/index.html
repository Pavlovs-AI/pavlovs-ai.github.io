Psychology is a science

Empirical approach: an evidence-based method that draws on observation and experimentation.

The study of psychology requires a scientific attitude. Testing of hypotheses is important in psychology.

Three key elements of the scientific attitude:

  1. Curiosity
  2. Skepticism
  3. Humility

Four main goals of psychology:

  1. Describe
  2. Explain
  3. Predict
  4. Change

History of psychology

The embryonic form of psychology can be dated back to before 300 B.C.E, philosopher Aristotle.

Birth of Psychology

In 1879, Wilhelm Wundt established the first psychology laboratory at the University of Leipzig, Germany. This marked the birth of psychology.

Wundt was seeking to measure “atoms of the mind”, which was the fastest and simplest mental processes. The machine measured how long it took for people to press a telegraph key after hearing a ball hit a platform.

Psychology was organized into different schools of thought. Two early schools were structuralism and functionalism.

Structuralism

Wundt and Titchener

Used introspection to reveal the structure of the human mind.

Titchener aimed to classify and understand elements of the mind’s structure, engaging people in self-reflective introspection. This focuses on the sensations, feelings, images and the relationship between them.

However, introspection was somewhat unreliable due to the individual differences of feelings and verbal ability. Structuralism waned as introspection failed.

Functionalism

Promoted by Jame; Influenced by Darwin

Explore how mental and behavioral processes function and contribute to adaptation, survival and flourishing.

William James considered the evolved functions beyond the inward thoughts of structuralism. He assumed that thinking developed as an adaptation which helped to survive and reproduce.

Calkins was the first female president of the American Psychological Association (APA).

Washburn was the first official female psychology Ph.D. who wrote The Animal Mind and became the second female president.

Development of Psychology

Psychology was defined as “the science of mental life” in the early development of psychology. Watson and Skinner dismissed introspection and redefined psychology as “the scientific study of observable behavior”.

Behaviorism

Watson and Skinner

  1. Psychology should be an objective science.

  2. Psychology studies behavior without reference to mental processes.

Behaviorism pointed out that sensations, feelings and thoughts were not observable, but behaviors are conditioned and can be observed and recorded, which respond to and learn in different situations.

In a controversial study on a baby who became famous as “Little Albert”, Watson and his companion Rayner showed that fear could be learned.

Freudian (Psychoanalytic) Psychology

Sigmund Freud

Emphasized the way our unconscious mind and childhood experiences affect our behavior.

Humanistic Psychology

Rogers and Maslow

Focused on growth potential, needs for love and acceptance, and the environments that nurture or limit personal growth.

Contemporary psychology

Psychologists in the 1960s pioneered a cognitive revolution simultaneous with humanistic psychology’s emergence. This led the field back to its early interest in how our mind processes and retained information.

Cognitive psychology the study of mental processes.

Cognitive neuroscience the interdisciplinary study of the brain activity linked with cognition.

Psychology is defined as “the science of behavior and mental process”.

  • Behavior: any action we can observe and record.
  • Mental processes: internal, subjective experiences.

Evolutionary Psychology and Behavioral Genetics

the nature-nurture issue argues is the longstanding controversy over the relative contributions that genes and experience make to the development of psychological traits and behaviors.

Nature: Plato, Descartes, etc.

Nurture: Aristotle, Locke, etc.

Darwin proposed the evolutionary process of natural selection, suggesting that nature selects traits that best enable an organism to survive and reproduce in a particular environment.

Evolutionary psychology how humans are alike because of common biology and evolutionary history.

Behavioral genetics how individuals differ because of differing genes and environments.

Twin studies: comparing the effects of nature and nurture of identical twins with fraternal twins.

Cross-Cultural and Gender Psychology

The interplay and controversy between culture and biological nature; Differences between gender identity and sex.

Positive Psychology

Martin Seligman

Research on human flourishing that help us to thrive.

Positive psychology aims to promote strengths and virtues that help individuals and communities to thrive.

Psychology’s Three Main Levels of Analysis

The biopsychosocial approach:

  1. Biological approach
  2. Psychological approach
  3. Sociocultural approach

Considers all biological, psychological and sociocultural approaches in analyzing a given behavior or mental process.

Perspective Focus
Neuroscience How the body and brain enable mental processes
Evolutionary How the natural selection of traits has promoted the survival of genes
Behavior genetics How genes and environment influence individual differences
Psychodynamic How behavior springs from unconscious drives and conflicts
Behavioral How we learn observable responses
Cognitive How we encode, process, store and retrieve information
Social-cultural How behavior and thinking vary across situations and cultures

Subfields of psychology focus on either basic research (pure science that aims to increase the scientific knowledge base) or applied research (scientific study that aims to solve practical problems).
